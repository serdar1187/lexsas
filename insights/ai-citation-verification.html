AI citation sanctions keep climbing: a verification protocol for legal teams

The counter only moves one way. Damien Charlotin's public database of court decisions engaging with AI-hallucinated filings passed 1,700 entries this month, the majority from US courts, with several new decisions added every working day.

The escalation is easy to trace. In 2023, Mata v. Avianca cost two lawyers $5,000 and made global news. In 2026 alone, the Sixth Circuit ordered two attorneys to pay $15,000 each on top of the opposing side's fees, an Oregon winery dispute produced roughly $110,000 in aggregate sanctions and a dismissal, and Nebraska suspended a lawyer from practice until further order of the court, pending full disciplinary proceedings, over AI-hallucinated citations. On 3 June, the Ninth Circuit suspended two lawyers from its bar for six months, fined them $2,500 each, and ordered them to disclose any AI use in their filings for the next two years.

Read the orders and one pattern holds. Courts do not sanction the use of AI; they sanction signing and filing what was not checked. The Ninth Circuit put it plainly: the rules are not violated at the point of research and drafting, but at the point of signing and filing. Severity then tracks the response. Lawyers who disclosed the error promptly and corrected it received warnings. Lawyers who blamed typos, juniors or another AI tool received suspensions. Hundreds of US judges also run their own standing orders on AI in filings, tracked by Ropes and Gray, so the local rule needs checking before every submission.

My verification protocol has five steps, none optional for anything that leaves the team. One, open every citation in the official reporter or database; never ask a second AI whether the first AI's case exists. Two, compare every quotation against the opinion word by word. Three, test the proposition: the case must actually say what the draft claims it says. Four, test validity: the authority must still be good law. Five, a named person signs the verification note before the document goes out. The same five steps apply to client memos and advice, not only to court filings.

In-house teams have one extra lever: outside counsel. Put in the engagement letter how the firm verifies AI-assisted citations and who certifies it per filing. Turkish bar guidance now lands in the same place: the Union of Turkish Bar Associations' advisory guide for lawyers, published in June 2026, puts the duty to verify AI output and the final responsibility squarely on the lawyer. The tool is welcome in the workflow. The judgment never leaves it.
