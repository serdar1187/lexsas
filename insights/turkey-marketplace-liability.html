Marketplace intermediary liability in Turkey: three regimes to read together

By Asim Serdar Yilmaz

I spent years running legal and regulatory work for online marketplaces in Turkey, and the question that came up most was deceptively simple. When a third party sells something on our platform, what are we on the hook for? The honest answer is that no single statute holds it. A marketplace's exposure sits where three regimes overlap, and the work is to read them as one map rather than three separate texts.

The first is the E-Commerce Law, No. 6563, reshaped by the 2022 amendments that introduced a tiered set of obligations for large intermediary service providers. The second is consumer protection under Law No. 6502, which sets duties around pre-contract information, the defective goods process, and refunds when a transaction goes wrong. The third is the internet content regime under Law No. 5651. Its main personal-rights takedown article was annulled by the Constitutional Court in October 2024 and is now awaiting re-legislation, which is exactly why a platform cannot read it in isolation and needs the combined map.

The volume behind these questions is not small. The Ministry of Trade reported total e-commerce volume in Turkey at 3 trillion lira in 2024, up 61.7 percent on the year (ticaret.gov.tr). More sellers and more listings mean more moments where the three regimes meet a single transaction.

The practical work is mapping them together. A single listing or complaint can raise duties under more than one regime at once: a takedown question, a consumer remedy, and a record-keeping obligation, side by side. Reading one regime in isolation is how a compliant-looking process still leaves a gap. The artifact that closes it is a current, per-regime duty map, each duty tied to an owner and a legal basis.
