The EU AI Act on 2 August 2026: what actually applies

By Asim Serdar Yilmaz

For two years, 2 August 2026 was the date every AI compliance plan revolved around. In June the calendar was redrawn: the Digital Omnibus, adopted by the European Parliament on 16 June and by the Council on 29 June, moves the high-risk obligations for Annex III systems to 2 December 2027 and the rules for AI embedded in regulated products to 2 August 2028. The act now awaits publication in the Official Journal.

The common misreading is to treat that as a pause. One block of the regulation was not postponed: the Article 50 transparency obligations, which apply from 2 August 2026 to any organisation whose AI interacts with people or publishes content.

In practice, three duties matter for most companies. A chatbot must disclose that the user is talking to AI at the start of the interaction. AI-generated text, image, audio and video must carry machine-readable marking. Deepfakes, and AI-generated text published to inform the public, must be visibly labelled. The Commission's Code of Practice on marking and labelling, published on 10 June 2026, is the ready-made path to show conformity.

Enforcement arrives on the same date. From 2 August 2026, national market surveillance authorities can fine breaches of these duties up to 15 million euro or 3 percent of worldwide annual turnover. Two narrow transitions exist: providers of generative systems already on the market have until 2 December 2026 to make machine-readable marking work, and a new ban on AI systems that generate non-consensual intimate imagery applies from that December date.

The rest of the timeline was already live. The prohibited-practice bans and the AI literacy duty have applied since February 2025, and the obligations for general-purpose AI models since August 2025, with the voluntary GPAI Code of Practice as the mainstream compliance route for providers.

For a legal team, three moves before the date. First, inventory every AI surface that talks to people or publishes outward, including marketing, customer service and product features. Second, check that disclosures are switched on and visible at first contact, not buried in terms of use. Third, write down for each surface whether you sit in the provider or deployer seat: both carry duties, and white-labelling a vendor's tool can move you into the provider one. The high-risk work bought time. Transparency did not.
