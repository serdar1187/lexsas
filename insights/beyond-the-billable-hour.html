The billable hour is running out of logic

The billable hour survived every earlier wave of legal technology because efficiency and revenue pulled in opposite directions: a faster lawyer earned less. Generative AI breaks that truce. Clio's Legal Trends Report estimates that around 74 percent of the work law firms currently bill by the hour is exposed to automation. When the hour itself deflates, pricing by the hour stops making sense.

Clients have already voted. In the same research, 71 percent say they would rather pay a flat fee for an entire matter than watch a meter run. A new generation of AI-native legal businesses, Eudia among the most visible, is building directly on that preference, selling defined outcomes at fixed fees rather than time.

What replaces the hour is a menu rather than a single model: flat fees per matter, subscriptions for ongoing counsel, value-based pricing for results. The common thread is that the client buys an outcome, and the efficiency gain belongs to whoever built the better workflow.

For firms the strategic point is blunt. Price the work, and your own automation becomes margin. Price the time, and it becomes your client's discount.
